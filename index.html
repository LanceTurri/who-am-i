Your CSS
Alright! Let's talk about CSS. I LOVE writing CSS.

My HTML and CSS writing philosophy (if you can call it that) is very simple, write as little as possible. Of course I subscribe to the DRY principle and the idea of OOCSS, but how my code actually turns out is always dependant on the project and what its needs are. The problem comes in that scalable CSS takes different forms depending on what you are trying to do and what scales for the company and product you are a part of. For instance, writing CSS when you are the only front end guy is quite different than having a team of font loving (My favorite is Avenir), pixel perfect counterparts.

At the end of the day, I want to write as clear and concise of front end code as possible. Someone reading what I've worked on should never have to ask me how it works, or why I did it that way (that's what comments are for!).

Your most complex problem

This is going to sound like a bit of a copout... but the most complex problem I've faced is writing scalable CSS. My definition of this is creating a structure that is both welcoming for developers and able to scale gracefully with new modules, widgets, features, and designs. And therein lies the challenge, to create something that is brilliantly concise and can grow at a whim into any number of things. The way that I do that is by thinking about the individual parts instead of the whole when I am writing code.

As a kid I used to build with Lego's every night. I would stay up till three in the morning (back when cartoon network went to static after Space Ghost), building everything I possibly could. When I would do this, I never thought I need a spaceship, I would say I need a 2 x 4 that is red to build this section. That's what CSS should do, building reusable, dynamic, agnostic blocks that can be combined to build anything. And when you just have to have that trapdoor so that the evil wizard can be defeated, you can create a new class.
